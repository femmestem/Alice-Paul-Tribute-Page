Alice Paul

Leading political figure who secured American women's right to vote

    Here's a Timeline of Alice Paul's Life

  • 1905 - Paul graduated from Swarthmore College and studied at the New York School of Social Work.
  • 1906 to 1909 - Paul was a social worker in England: jailed three times for her suffragist efforts.
  • 1912 - Paul became head of the National American Woman Suffrage Association's congressional committee.
  • 1913 - Paul helped form the Congressional Union for Woman Suffrage.
  • 1917 - The organization joined the Woman's Party to create the National Woman's Party. Paul was jailed three more times for her fight. The NWP staged the 1st political protest to picket the White House, demanding the right to vote.
  • 1920 - The Nineteenth Amendment was ratified, and Paul earned a law degree from Washington College of Law, and MA and Ph.D. degrees from American University.
  • 1923 - Paul wrote an equal rights amendment to the Constitution and introduced it in Congress. The measure failed and Paul began supporting the League of Nations.
  • 1938 - Paul founded the World Party for Equal Rights for Women, known as the World Women's Party.
  • 1942 - Paul was elected to chair the National Woman's Party.
  • 1964 - Paul successfully lobbied for wording on gender equality to be included in the preamble to the United Nations Charter and in the Civil Rights Act of 1964.
  • 1977 - Paul continued fighting for equal rights until she suffered a debilitating stroke in 1974, and passed away in at age 92 at a nursing home less than a mile from her childhood home.
  • 1979 - Alice Paul was inducted posthumously into the National Women's Hall of Fame.
